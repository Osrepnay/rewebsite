Public Forum Dictionary

Debaters often speak an unintelligible language. This guide can help you navigate any jargon you might come across.

A

Affirmative: The side that affirms the resolution. Generally called the "aff" or the "pro" side.

Affirmative Case/Constructive: The speech during which the affirmative team provides a set of arguments discussing the advantages of the resolution. Often referred to as the 1AC or 2AC depending on whether the affirmative team is speaking first or second.

Argument: A reason for affirming or negating the resolution. Generally consists of a claim, warrant, and impact.

A2/AT: An abbreviation for "answer to"; often used in briefs and block files.

B

Ballot: The judge's vote for who wins the round. Often used when referring to the judge's decision in-round.

Bid: A "point" awarded to debaters for reaching a certain elimination round in select tournaments. In Public Forum, two bids are necessary to qualify for the Tournament of Champions.

Break: See elimination round

Brief: Professionaly prepared topic analysis, cases, cards, and blockfiles. May be free or require purchase.

C

Card: A piece of evidence, adapted for debate. Generally includes the citation (publication date and author name), source, and the actual text, with important parts highlighted.

Card-cutting: The act of finding evidence and highlighting parts related to the topic to create a card.

Case: The arguments given by a team to support their position as the affirmative or negative. Generally also includes a framework.

Claim: The overall structure of an argument; a brief summarization of the impact.

Con: See negative.

Constructive speech: One of the first two speeches in Public Forum; a four-minute speech during which the speaking team's first speaker delivers their case.

Contention: See argument.

Crossfire: A three-minute questioning period during which both teams' first or second speaker ask each other questions.

D

Double turn: A mistake in which one reads both an impact turn and a link turn, negating the effects of both.

Drop: An argument that is not addressed by the opponent team. Similarly, to "drop" an argument means to not respond to one of your opponents' contentions, either deliberately or unintentionally.

E

Elimination Round (Elim): Rounds held to determine the winner of a tournament; losing means elimination from the tournament, hence the name.

F

Fiat: The concept that in the affirmative world, the resolution is executed completely, regardless of how likely it is to occur in the real world.

First speaker: One of the two speakers on a team. Delivers the constructive speech and the summary speech.

Flashing: The act of sending your cases to your opponents before the round so they can read over the evidence without having to call for it. At the time of writing, not very common in Public Forum. Referred to as "flashing" because it used to entail passing a physical flash drive to your opponents.

Flow: Notes taken by debaters or flow judges during the round. Also used to refer to the structure of arguments and responses in the debate.

Flow judge: A judge who is experienced with debate and generally votes off the flow, hence their name.

Framework: The way a team believes a judge should measure the impacts in round.

G

Grand Crossfire: A special crossfire during which both teams' first and second speakers can ask questions.

H

Hegemon: An entity (often a state or organization) that has hegemony over others.

Hegemony: Influence, control, or authority over other actors.

I

Impact: The final part of an argument; the result of affirming or negating the resolution.

Impact calculus: The act of comparing impacts, generally based on magnitude, probability, and timeframe.

Impact turn: A turn in which you demonstrate that your opponents' argument's impact results in another impact that supports your side.

J

Judge: The person who decides which side wins the round; or, in the case of rounds with multiple judges, part of the panel which votes on the winner.

L

Lay judge: A judge who has little experience with debate. May vote off articulation or even clothing rather than the flow. Often used to refer to parent judges.

Link: The connection(s) between the resolution and the impact. Generally supported with analysis or evidence.

Link turn: A turn in which you demonstrate that your opponents' argument's link results in an impact that supports your side.

M

Magnitude: The potential size of an impact. Important when performing impact calculus.

N

Negative: The side that negates the resolution. Generally called the "neg" or the "con" side.

Nonunique: The state of occurring in the status quo already, making the issue irrelevant since it will occur regardless.

P

Preliminary Round (Prelim): Rounds held to determine which teams advance to the elimination rounds. Generally the first few rounds of a tournament.

Pro: See affirmative.

Probability: The likelihood of an impact occurring. Important when performing impact calculus.

R

Resolution: The topic of the debate; a statement which the affirmative team supports and the negative team negates.

S

Second speaker: One of the two speakers on a team. Delivers the rebuttal speech and the Final Focus speech.

Status quo: A Latin phrase meaning the current state of affairs; the pre-resolution world.

T

Timeframe: The timeframe in which an impact may occur. Important when performing impact calculus.

Tournament of Champions (TOC): One of the most prestigious debate tournaments in the United States. Hosted by the University of Kentucky on the last weekend of April. To qualify, a team must earned at least two bids.

Topic: See resolution.

Turn: A response to your opponents' arguments which proves that either their link or their impact benefits your side.

U

Uniqueness: Whether an impact is unique to one side, or whether it already occurs in the status quo (making the issue nonunique).

W

Warrant: The reason the claim of an argument would be true.
